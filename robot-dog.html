Microprocessor Controlled Systems

Quadruped Robot Dog

A one-semester project that turned a legged-robot idea into a working prototype through mechanical design, 3D printing, embedded control, inverse kinematics, servo calibration, and repeated integration testing.

Project Facts

Role
Team lead, mechanical designer, controls developer
Hardware
ESP32, PCA9685 servo driver, eight MG90S servos, 3D-printed structure
Software
MATLAB gait simulation, Arduino implementation, mobile web control interface
Result
Walking, standing, sitting, waving, and rocking motions; course grade A+

Project Goal

The assignment was not only to assemble a robot, but to build a coordinated mechanical and control system. Our team chose a quadruped robot because it forced us to connect structure, actuator placement, power routing, kinematics, and timing. As team lead, I organized the work so mechanical design, electronics, and code could be tested together instead of waiting until the final week for integration.

I designed the body and leg modules around maintainability. The robot needed to be easy to assemble, repair, and modify after tests revealed problems. This meant thinking about screw access, servo replacement, wire routing, battery placement, and the relationship between the center of gravity and the leg workspace.

Key Engineering Decisions

Modular legs

The leg structure was designed as replaceable modules so a damaged print or failed servo would not require rebuilding the whole robot.

Simulation first

I tested gait logic and inverse kinematics in MATLAB before moving the equations into the Arduino program.

Practical packaging

The electronics layout considered wire length, battery access, board placement, and weight distribution rather than only fitting parts into the body.

Control and Debugging Process

  1. Defined the leg geometry and servo directions, then calibrated each motor so commanded angles matched the physical configuration.
  2. Built foot trajectories and inverse-kinematics calculations in MATLAB to check whether the leg path stayed inside the reachable workspace.
  3. Translated the model into Arduino code for the ESP32 and PCA9685, separating gait update logic, foot trajectory generation, angle calculation, and servo output.
  4. Tested simple actions first, then combined them into more complex motions after the robot could reliably stand and shift weight.

What This Project Shows

  • I can design a mechanical system while anticipating assembly and repair problems.
  • I can move from mathematical simulation to embedded implementation on real hardware.
  • I can lead a team through integration problems where mechanical and software causes overlap.
  • I understand that legged robots require geometry, timing, calibration, and structure to work together.
