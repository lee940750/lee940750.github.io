Machine Design Theory

Foldable Long-Distance Ball Transfer Mechanism

A deployable mechanism designed to move a ball between platforms while fitting within strict size limits, maintaining enough stiffness after deployment, and remaining repairable during repeated testing.

Project Facts

Role
Team lead, concept designer, fabrication planner
Materials
3D-printed parts, MDF structure, hinges, latch hardware, hand-built assemblies
Main Constraint
Compact storage size with reliable deployment and ball transfer
Result
Stable final mechanism with strong final-test performance

Project Goal

The Machine Design Theory project required a mechanism that could transfer a ball from a lower platform to a higher platform while staying within a specified length, width, and height. The basic movement of picking up and releasing a ball was only one part of the problem. The harder requirement was packaging the system into the allowed size and then deploying it reliably.

As team lead, I guided the team from early concepts to fabrication. I focused on turning the size constraint into a practical folding architecture, then checking whether the deployed structure would be stiff enough to perform the task repeatedly.

Design Decisions

Folding as the core problem

I treated the folded-to-deployed transition as a main design requirement, not a secondary packaging detail.

Simple hardware adaptation

Door hinges and latch hardware became a reliable folding module after early custom ideas proved less smooth.

Fabrication-aware structure

3D-printed components and MDF were chosen and arranged around available tools, assembly speed, and repairability.

Problem Solving Process

  1. Broke the task into storage, deployment, ball pickup, ball transport, and release, then evaluated how each function affected the others.
  2. Tested folding concepts early because a mechanism that only works in its deployed form would fail the size constraint.
  3. Replaced unreliable folding ideas with hinge-and-latch modules that provided smoother motion and clearer locked positions.
  4. Adjusted structure and fabrication details after considering strength, stiffness, weight, and the ability to repair parts before final evaluation.

Engineering Takeaways

This project was valuable because it rewarded practical mechanical judgment. The best solution was not necessarily the most complex one; it was the one that could be made, assembled, adjusted, and trusted in a final test. I learned to evaluate a design not only by its ideal motion, but also by how it behaves during setup, deployment, and repeated use.

  • Packaging constraints can dominate mechanism architecture.
  • Accessible off-the-shelf hardware can be an excellent engineering solution.
  • Stiffness after deployment is as important as smooth folding motion.
