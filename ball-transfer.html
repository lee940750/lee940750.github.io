Case Study | Deployable Mechanism

Foldable Long-Distance Ball Transfer Mechanism

A compact deployable mechanism designed around packaging, stiffness, reliable deployment, and repeatable ball transfer under real fabrication constraints.

Challenge Move a ball between platforms while fitting within strict storage limits.
My Contribution Led concept design, folding architecture, fabrication planning, and iteration.
Technical Stack 3D-printed parts, MDF structure, hinges, latch hardware, repeated test builds.
Outcome Stable final mechanism with reliable deployment and strong final-test performance.
Role Team lead, concept designer, fabrication planner
Materials 3D-printed parts, MDF structure, hinges, latch hardware
Constraint Compact storage size with reliable deployment and transfer
Result Stable final mechanism with strong final-test performance

Treated packaging as the main engineering problem.

The mechanism had to pick up, transport, and release a ball while staying inside a fixed envelope. I treated folding, deployment, lock-up stiffness, and repair access as primary design requirements rather than details to solve later.

This shifted the project from simply making a transfer path to designing a compact machine that could repeatedly move from storage state to working state.

Used simple hardware to improve reliability.

Early folding concepts were not dependable enough. I adapted accessible hinges and latch hardware into a clearer folding module, making the motion path more predictable and the locked state more stable.

Balanced ideal motion with buildable mechanics.

The final design emphasized practical reliability over unnecessary complexity. It performed dependably in final evaluation and strengthened my ability to connect mechanism architecture with fabrication, assembly order, and test behavior.

  • Packaging constraints can dominate mechanism architecture.
  • Off-the-shelf hardware can solve serious design problems when used carefully.
  • Repair access and assembly order directly affect final reliability.

Engineering flow

01 Start from the envelope

Made storage size and deployment requirements drive the mechanism architecture.

02 Prototype foldable concepts

03 Adapt reliable hardware

Used hinges and latch hardware to create a clearer motion and locked state.

04 Test repeatable transfer

Iterated toward a final mechanism that deployed and transferred reliably.

Reviewer takeaway

This project demonstrates engineering judgment in a less glamorous but very important area: making mechanisms fit, deploy, lock, and survive real fabrication constraints. That judgment transfers directly to robot hardware design, where packaging and serviceability often decide whether a system can be tested enough to improve.
