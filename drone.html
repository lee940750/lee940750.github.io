Practice of Mechanical Engineering

Quadcopter Ball-Transfer Drone

A full-system drone project for transporting a ball from a low platform to a high platform, developed through airframe design, propulsion testing, controller integration, and final-test risk management.

Role Team lead, system designer, integration coordinator
Scope Airframe, ball mechanism, electronics, power, thrust testing
Debugging Motor behavior, SBUS signal chain, flight controller, resonance
Result Full marks in midterm and final tests; course grade A+

A mission-driven drone, not only a flying frame.

The project required a drone to move a ball between platforms, so the mechanical design, payload placement, power system, and flight stability had to be considered together. As team lead, I coordinated concept selection, procurement, fabrication, testing, and final evaluation strategy.

The ball-transfer mechanism affected mass distribution and flight behavior, so we treated the airframe and payload system as one integrated design problem.

Testing thrust, signals, and stability before the final run.

We used thrust-to-throttle testing to evaluate whether the selected motors, propellers, ESCs, and battery could provide enough control margin. During integration, we separated motor, signal, mechanical vibration, and flight-controller causes instead of treating every instability as a tuning problem.

Risk control for a one-shot evaluation.

Because the final evaluation had limited formal attempts, our team prepared spare assemblies, reviewed the rules carefully, and planned likely failure responses. The final result was one of the most stable systems in the course.

  • Stable flight depends on structure, signals, power, and tuning working together.
  • Final-test reliability improves when spare parts and fallback plans are ready.
  • Leadership means turning uncertain test data into clear next actions.
