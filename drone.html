Practice of Mechanical Engineering

Quadcopter Ball-Transfer Drone

A full-system drone project for transporting a ball from a low platform to a high platform, developed through airframe design, propulsion testing, controller integration, debugging, and final-test risk management.

Project Facts

Role
Team lead, system designer, integration and testing coordinator
Scope
Airframe, ball mechanism, electronics, power supply, thrust testing, flight stability
Debugging
Motor behavior, SBUS signal chain, flight controller setup, resonance and stability
Result
Full marks in midterm and final tests; course grade A+

Project Goal

The final required project in Practice of Mechanical Engineering was to build a drone that could move a ball from a low platform to a higher platform. This was not a pure flight project: the vehicle had to generate enough thrust, remain stable, carry or guide the ball reliably, and complete the task under competition-like evaluation rules.

As team lead, I coordinated the schedule from concept selection to final testing. I also helped connect the mechanical and electrical decisions: airframe geometry affected vibration, component placement affected stability, power supply affected motor behavior, and the ball-transfer mechanism affected the payload and center of gravity.

System-Level Work

Airframe and payload

The frame and ball-handling concept were evaluated together because a good mechanism could still make the drone unstable if it shifted mass poorly.

Thrust testing

We used thrust-to-throttle data to estimate whether the vehicle had enough control margin instead of relying only on rough motor specifications.

One-shot final risk

Because the final evaluation had limited attempts, we prepared spare assemblies and tested likely failure cases before the official run.

Debugging and Validation

  1. Checked whether selected motors, propellers, ESCs, and battery choices could provide enough thrust and stable response for the added ball-transfer task.
  2. Investigated flight-controller and SBUS integration so receiver commands could be interpreted correctly and consistently.
  3. Diagnosed motor-related problems by separating electrical, signal, and mechanical causes rather than treating every instability as a tuning issue.
  4. Looked for resonance and structural vibration sources that could disturb sensors or make flight control less predictable.
  5. Prepared a final-test strategy with spare components, rule-based contingency plans, and clear team responsibilities.

Leadership Lessons

This project taught me that leading a hardware team is largely about making uncertainty visible. I needed to turn open-ended problems into short tests, decide what evidence was good enough to move forward, and keep team members aligned when design, electronics, and testing tasks depended on each other.

  • Stable flight is a system result, not a single tuning parameter.
  • Risk management matters when the final demonstration has limited attempts.
  • Good team leadership includes preparing backup options before the machine fails.
