Project Summary

Designed and implemented a small quadruped robot platform that connects mechanical structure, leg kinematics, gait planning, and motor control. The project focused on making a physical robot walk reliably while solving real integration issues such as asymmetric torque, leg lift height, backward drift, and center-of-mass stability during single-leg gestures.

Problem and Motivation

Legged locomotion is challenging because the robot must coordinate mechanical geometry, actuator limitations, contact timing, and body stability. The goal of this project was to build a compact quadruped platform that could perform basic forward and backward walking while remaining mechanically simple and low-cost.

The project also explored how gait parameters affect stability, including foot trajectory height, step length, phase timing, and weight shifting.

Technical Approach

  • Leg kinematics: Defined a geometric model for each leg and mapped desired foot positions to motor angles through inverse kinematics.
  • Gait generation: Tuned step length, foot lift height, stance timing, and leg phase differences for forward and backward motion.
  • Motor control: Integrated actuator commands and adjusted tracking behavior through repeated physical testing.
  • Stability debugging: Reduced backward walking drift and improved gesture stability by modifying gait parameters and shifting the body posture.

Results and Engineering Iteration

The robot was tested through walking and gesture motions. Several issues were identified during testing, including rear-leg lift problems, rightward drift during backward walking, and instability while raising the left front leg. These problems were treated as engineering debugging tasks rather than only software errors.

Key improvements included adjusting the rear-leg trajectory, compensating asymmetric motion during backward walking, and shifting the center of mass toward the right-rear side during the waving motion.

Reflection

This project showed that legged robotics is not only a kinematics problem. Stable walking depends on the interaction between actuator torque, mechanical compliance, foot contact, body posture, and gait timing. The most valuable part of the project was learning how to debug a complete physical robot by connecting observed motion errors to mechanical and control-level causes.
